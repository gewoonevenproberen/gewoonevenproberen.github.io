Why your brain always gets the number wrong

8 min read · Interactive · March 2026

The setup

Every day, humans make estimates. How long will this take? How many people were there? How much does that cost? We're constantly converting the vague and unknown into a working number — and almost universally, we get it wrong in the same direction: too high.

This isn't random error. It's systematic. Psychologists have studied it for decades across cultures, professions, and contexts. The phenomenon appears in project planning, medical diagnosis, financial forecasting, and even something as simple as estimating how many items are in a jar.

"The first step to thinking clearly is admitting that your gut is a terrible statistician."

In this essay, we'll put your intuition to the test — then show you a simple statistical method that systematically outperforms human estimation. The gap between the two might surprise you.

Why statistics beats intuition

Our brains use heuristics — mental shortcuts — to produce estimates quickly. The problem is these shortcuts are calibrated for ancestral environments, not for large numbers or complex systems. When we try to estimate things beyond our direct experience, we anchor on the wrong reference points and scale poorly.

A simple base-rate decomposition — also called a Fermi estimation — outperforms expert intuition in dozens of documented studies. The idea: break any unknown into observable components, estimate each independently, then multiply.

What's actually happening in your brain

When you made your guess above, you likely did one of two things: you either pictured a scene — a busy street, cyclists passing — and extrapolated from that image. Or you anchored on a number you'd heard before and adjusted slightly.

Both strategies suffer from the same flaw: they rely on availability, not base rates. Your estimate reflects what's vivid and memorable, not what's statistically representative. People who live near a bike lane overestimate cycling rates. People who rarely see cyclists underestimate.

The Fermi method forces you to leave your subjective frame of reference and work from population-level numbers. Each variable is smaller and more tractable. The errors in each component partially cancel out. The result is reliably closer to truth.
